A Buddhist shrine on the side of a road is often called a "stupa" or a "wayside shrine." In different cultures and contexts, these shrines might have specific names:

* Stupa: In many Buddhist traditions, a stupa is a mound-like structure containing relics and is used as a place of meditation. While stupas are typically larger structures, smaller versions can sometimes be found as roadside shrines.
* Wayside Shrine: This is a general term used to describe small shrines located along roadsides. These shrines can contain images or statues of the Buddha and serve as a place for travelers to stop, offer prayers, and meditate.
* Jizo Shrine: In Japanese Buddhism, a common roadside shrine is dedicated to Jizo (Kṣitigarbha), a bodhisattva who is believed to protect travelers, children, and the souls of the deceased. These shrines often feature statues of Jizo and are found at crossroads, paths, and roadsides.
* Chorten: In Tibetan Buddhism, a chorten is a type of stupa that serves as a shrine. They can be found in various sizes, including smaller ones that might be located by roadsides.
* Pindola Shrine: In some East Asian countries, roadside shrines may be dedicated to Pindola Bharadvaja, one of the sixteen arhats, known for protecting travelers.

These shrines provide a spiritual sanctuary for travelers and passersby, offering a place for reflection, prayer, and connection with the sacred.
