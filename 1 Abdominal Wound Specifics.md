**Things you probably should delete from your browser history after you research them: “How immediately fatal is a sword strike to the abdomen?”**

If a sword goes into the belly, three broad outcomes usually decide how fast someone dies:

* First, major blood vessel hit. If the blade severs something like the abdominal aorta or one of its big branches, that’s catastrophic. Massive internal bleeding can drop someone in seconds to a couple of minutes. They may still be conscious briefly---long enough for a dramatic exchange, if you’re writing---but they’re not walking that off. This is the “lights fading, knees buckle” version.
* Second, organ damage without immediate vascular destruction. Most abdominal stabs in history fell into this category. Intestines, liver, stomach---messy, extremely painful, but not instantly fatal. The liver bleeds a lot; the intestines don’t kill you right away but set you up for infection. In a pre-modern setting, this often meant death hours to days later from internal bleeding or peritonitis (infection in the abdominal cavity). The person could fight for a short time, especially on adrenaline. There are documented cases of soldiers continuing to function briefly after gut wounds.
* Third, superficial or glancing wounds. A slash across the abdomen that doesn’t penetrate deeply can be survivable, especially with modern medicine. Historically, survival odds drop without surgery and antibiotics.

A few practical storytelling notes:

* Abdominal wounds are usually agonizing. Shock sets in quickly.
* There’s often a delayed collapse rather than instant death unless a major vessel is cut.
* If the intestines are perforated, infection becomes the ticking clock. That’s a slower, grim fate.
* A clean thrust and withdrawal bleeds differently from a twist-and-tear motion.

Ursza with Gu’Senlin discipline would know exactly where to strike for immediacy. A warrior trained to kill efficiently wouldn’t “just stab the belly” unless the goal was slow incapacitation. If she wanted instant lethality, she’d target the heart, throat, or major vessels with precision. A messy abdominal thrust suggests rage, chaos, or improvisation.

If Serah (under Ursza’s madness) drives a blade into Rik’s abdomen hard enough to drop him, several plausible things could happen that would stop or nearly stop his heart without immediately killing him.

* One: massive blood loss from a liver or major vessel nick. The liver is a blood sponge. A deep thrust into the right upper quadrant could cause rapid internal bleeding. Blood pressure crashes. When blood pressure drops low enough, the heart can go into arrhythmia or cardiac arrest. That can look like his heart “skipping” or stopping long enough to trigger a biometric dead man’s switch.
* Two: vagal shock. A deep abdominal strike can stimulate the vagus nerve reflexively. Sudden intense trauma in the abdomen can cause bradycardia --- the heart rate plummets. In rare cases, it can flatline briefly. This is more believable if it’s described as his heart “bottoming out” rather than being cleanly pierced.
* Three: commotio-type arrhythmia from impact. Less likely from abdomen than chest, but severe trauma can destabilize the electrical rhythm. He could drop into ventricular tachycardia or fibrillation briefly. That’s dramatic and reversible with immediate intervention.

In order for him to technically “die” for a few seconds to set off the dead man’s switch, that’s medically plausible if he experiences:

* Severe hemorrhagic shock
* Brief cardiac arrhythmia
* Loss of perfusion leading to momentary arrest

In order for Serah to stabilize him long enough to reach a hospital, then she cannot have:

* Fully transected the aorta
* Completely destroyed the liver
* Severed the inferior vena cava

Any of those are game over in minutes without surgical theater-level intervention.

So narratively, the sweet spot is: She stabs him deep. He collapses. His implant reads “no pulse” or dangerously low perfusion and triggers the dead man’s switch protocol. She realizes what she’s done. Ursza’s fury recedes just enough. She applies medical stasis to keep him alive long enough to reach a hospital.

From a physiological standpoint, someone can survive:

* 2--4 minutes without effective circulation before brain injury risk rises
* Longer if there’s intermittent perfusion or rapid intervention

So if his heart “stops” for 10--20 seconds, enough to trigger a failsafe, that’s believable. Especially if it’s not literal flatline but below threshold detection.

If the wound is slightly to the side. Then it doesn’t risk aortal tear. Right upper quadrant hits liver. Left side hits spleen (also bleeds like fury but sometimes survivable). Lower abdomen hits intestines --- slower death, less immediate collapse.

So, we go with upper abdomen, heavy bleeding, cardiac instability, temporary arrest, revival.
