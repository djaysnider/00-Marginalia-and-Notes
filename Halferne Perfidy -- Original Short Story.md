The Halferne Perfidy

By Darrin Snider

"For now, this fearful night,

There is no stir or walking in the streets,

And the complexion of the element 

In favor's like the work we have in hand,

Most bloody, fiery, and most terrible." 

\-- Cassius--Julius Caesar, Act 1, Scene 3

The din of the street market was an unholy incantation to chaos itself. Street vendors, peddlers, zealots, and highwaymen converged upon it and mingled amongst themselves until they were indistinguishable from one another. It was winter, and that meant sub-zero temperatures, a constant blanket of snow and slush on the ground, and an unceasing wind that whipped between buildings and stung any patch of skin left bare. Still, despite the abundance of large disused structures in the district, the market was set up outdoors in the streets and alleyways. It was probably the only tradition Notosia still held. It was a sign of the times. Notosia had never exactly been a hospitable place to live--just a botched social experiment set up by Mother Earth and then left to its own devices when it proved no longer useful.

They were called political agitators by the legitimate world government back on Earth--people who refused to conform and play by the rules of the so-called "polite society." The few that allowed themselves to be captured endured the feigned pity of the people, the government, and the rule of laws they refused to acknowledge. Their own inhuman stubbornness and self-righteousness resisted all attempts to reform, rehabilitate, or, at the very least, silence them. 

The cruel irony was that those very same laws turned out to be their greatest ally. Earth's enlightened culture was based on tolerance and steadfastly refused to completely silence any undesirable element, even one so unapologetically dedicated to its downfall. Even as prisoners of the state, they were guaranteed a voice. They were handed the chance to make their case and tell their side of the story--and tell that story they did. 

Volumes of treatises, manifestos, and commentaries were published from the prisons. Eloquent words were daggers in the breastplates of the magnanimous World Order. They told how the government had succumbed to corruption through society's complacency. They told how religion, morality, and humanity were nothing more than quaint, old-fashioned concepts --- just words on pages that were read and quoted to the letter, but never really understood or practiced. They told how Earth had lost its way, a slowly dying dragon in a long-forgotten dream. Certainly, this had been a rallying cry for fanatics, megalomaniacs, and madmen for centuries, yet once in a while a special kind of madman arises, one that carries a certain spark about himself--one that inspires hope, confidence, or at the very least, fear in those he meets. These are the dangerous ones, for whether their beliefs are right or wrong, they inevitably shape the world.

Charisma is a powerful trait that carries ideas further than the truth alone. Nearly everyone read or listened to at least some of the "new thoughts" coming from the "correctional institutions", and many were starting to believe. Policies were questioned, changes were made, and grand schemes were adjusted. The ultimate impact may have only seemed infinitesimal at the time, but a wise man once said that even the beat of the smallest butterfly's wings can spark a hurricane somewhere in the world. Before too long, it was obvious that something needed to be done.

Leadership and power rarely walk hand in hand. Many who wield power are, in reality, very poor leaders; others who are natural leaders lack the power in any real fashion. The sole aim of the former is to prevent the transfer of that power--perceived or actual--to the latter. And to that end, there is one indisputable truth held above all others: only leaders can become martyrs. Therefore, it was decided to give those dissidents what they craved most: a chance to mold a world in their own image. 

That world was Notosia.

The problem with changing the world is simply that it takes effort, and sadly, the dissidents were closer to the truth than they knew. Earth had indeed become complacent. Few were prepared to make the sacrifices necessary to achieve their lofty dreams of a better world. When the call went out for followers to help with the formation of a new colony, only a fraction of the number required answered, and most of those were criminals, left-behinds, and social outcasts for whom even the unqualified hell of a fledgling colony world would be an improvement on the conditions they faced at home. The rest were little more than the weak-minded followers of madmen; they hung on their every word as if it were indisputable gospel, but offered no real contribution of their own.

All of these were our forefathers.

This having been said, it seems unlikely that any semblance of order would have taken root on this inhospitable mudball. Yet it did --- after a fashion. Notosia saw three governments in its first decade. When order, such as it was, was finally established, it was by the only means that our forbearers understood--the rule of might. Over the next two centuries, twenty-seven other governments saw their day in the sun, only to be cast down by the same accusations once levied at Earth: greed, corruption, and complacency.

As descendants, we have not evolved much beyond those humble beginnings. If history is the basis of cultural identity, then we are the blank slate. For history is inevitably written by the winners, and on Notosia there are no winners--at least none that have laid claim to the title for more than a few years at a time. To the casual observer, our world would seem like anarchy, yet to those who live here, the reality is far more pathetic. The populace of Notosia falls into one of two rigid categories: Those who act to bring about a new, better government, and those who passively observe the ebb and flow of power around them, content in what someone else decides is best for them, believing that there is nothing anyone can do about it. Even in the speckled crowd of the marketplace, it was easy to tell who was who.

I ignored most of the gatherers as I made my way through the streets to the modest hostelry I was instructed to watch. Fortunately, my reputation and physical presence are slightly more menacing than my actual tendencies, and the vast majority of the lowlifes in the city go out of their way to avoid me. Put simply, I am an activist--a servant of the people who understands that the only way to bring about meaningful change on this miserable planet is to fight for it centimeter by centimeter. To the current government, I am a terrorist; in my mind, I am a soldier; in the voice of history, I will be a hero--at least for a little while.

A blast of welcome heat greeted me as the door opened. The noonday sun shone through the entryway, its brightness reflecting off the pale, zombie-like faces of the patrons within. I took a seat at a table against the far wall and ordered a meal that was, true to some immutable law of social dynamics, far superior to any I would have found in the more affluent quarters of the city. Once finished, I ignored the stares and polite suggestions from the patrons to move along and busied myself by pretending to read the latest headlines and editorial commentaries on my datapad.

After two hours of waiting, she finally came down from her room. I had already seen her in an enhanced long-range surveillance photo--good enough to get a general idea of what she looked like, but nothing that could have prepared me for an honest, in-the-flesh sight of her. Anybody could see she didn't belong on this planet. She had the wrong build, the wrong coloring, the wrong clothes, and the look of life about her. She was tall for a female, almost 1.8 meters, I had been informed. Tresses of blonde hair framed an angular face beset with exotically shaped eyes of the deepest blue fire. She wore a form-fitting crimson leather tunic and gray pants adorned with a red, vein-like pattern. A dark green cloak was fastened loosely around her neck, concealing the hardware and weaponry she no doubt carried with her. She moved with the angular grace and balance of some predatory animal as she made her way through the room. 

Her name was Ursza Venter, and from what I had read, she was the most dangerous person on the planet.

Her eyes darted around the room, no doubt making a quick study and assessment of each inhabitant. I was told her kind could size you up with the quickest glance--what weapons you were likely to carry, how well you fought with them, and where your weaknesses lay. They say her kind never raises a hand unless they already know how to defeat you. I tried to study her as she studied the others in the room, but saw only the conflicting images: the totality of a warrior, trained in a way of life almost from birth, and carried in the body of a twenty-year-old girl.

I didn't think she had noticed me at all as she quietly sat down at a table no more than three meters from mine. Two men, obviously locals, nodded politely. She smiled slightly, leaned forward, and began speaking in soft tones. Her face was expressionless, her lips hardly moved, and despite my best efforts, I could only make out the faintest timbre of her voice over the background noise. Not that I needed to know what she was saying. I had already been informed that those two would be free traders, and she would be trying to secure a discreet passage off the planet. I also knew that she wouldn't be getting it.

I cleared the newsfeed from the datapad in front of me, opened a connection to a database at Nik Chel Imports, and placed a quick order for what--to the every-prying eyes of the government at least--would look like a simple order for twenty-nine kegs of what I had been told was a fine red wine from the south of France back on Earth. It took only five seconds for the screen to flicker, automatically handshaking a secure connection to a private terminal that most people would kill me to learn the location of. A small timer ticked in the bottom-right corner of the screen, indicating the connection could remain active for another 37 seconds while our beloved government hastily tried to trace the endpoints. Flipping the pad to scan mode, I took a quick capture of the two men and transmitted it. By the time the timer had ticked down to three seconds, a response was sent, and the connection was automatically severed. I read the words quickly before turning the datapad off: Freighter Captain Dom Biery and first mate--no political ties to speak of, and aside from a few minor "manifest inconsistencies," legitimate businessmen.

I watched the proceedings for approximately ten minutes. While Ursza's face remained completely passive, the other two were growing visibly agitated. Not surprisingly, most honest traders don't want anything to do with harboring fugitives--at least not on Notosia. Suddenly, her eyes became pleading. The look seemed unnatural on that face--a bad imitation of helplessness. She retrieved a small pouch from her waist and tossed it on the table. The unmistakable noise almost silenced the room. The two men shifted and looked around nervously. Hard currency was dangerous to carry in this part of the city, but she had enough goldslips in that bag to book a stateroom on a luxury liner. It was a severe miscalculation, and one that to most people would prove fatal. The two men quickly shook their heads, stood up, and exited the restaurant apologetically, as if expecting a bomb to go off any minute.

She sat for a moment, puzzled and alone, no doubt wondering where she had gone wrong. A small group of ruffians approached her: three men dressed like common street thugs. The largest of the three, obviously the leader, made a rather loud inquiry about how she would spend her evening and how he might help her spend some of the small fortune she was carrying. She said nothing, collected her belongings, and got up to leave. The other two quickly darted around her to cut short her exit. The big one chortled in a deep baritone growl that made my legs tremble. Ursza said nothing but arched one eyebrow and gave him a look back over her shoulder that should have made entire planets shift orbits to get out of her way.

It didn't do much good.

Like a wild animal, enraged at the challenge, the big man stood up and pointed a finger at her. I made out a few rather uncomplimentary remarks about her social habits as well as a particularly nasty threat if she didn't sit back down. She still stood passively, her face unchanging, eyes locked on the big man in some sort of feral challenge. 

He nodded to his partners, who reached out and grabbed her by the shoulder. Instantly, she crouched, disappearing momentarily from my view. When she reappeared, she was no longer wearing the bulky, heavy cloak and was standing between her two assailants. With no wasted motion whatsoever, she struck the first man in the throat, collapsing his windpipe. He gurgled his surprise and stumbled backward into the crowd. A swift side kick to the groin ensured that the other would be of no further concern.

Enraged, the leader drew a pistol from his coat--a slug-thrower, not particularly eloquent, but cheap to own and easy to maintain. Instantly, Ursza thrust her left hand out, palm extended. Two loud blasts made everyone in the room instantly dive for cover. The shots were fired at no more than a meter's distance yet, miraculously--or perhaps not so miraculously--both seemed to miss their intended target completely. Then, there was a flash of steel as a sword arced through the air and neatly separated the gun-wielder from his forearm. Both fell to the floor silently, the big man in obvious shock and surprise.

Ursza's young face was completely expressionless as she pulled a microfiber cloth from her pocket, wiped the long, curved blade with one quick stroke, and returned it to the sheath that was now plainly visible on her back. She was throwing the cape back around her shoulders with a none-too-subtle flourish as the screams of terrified patrons began filling the air. She paid them no heed as she retrieved the rest of her belongings. Her movements were so subtle, and her face so dispassionate, that I almost failed to notice the flick of her left wrist that sent a flashcap sailing to the far corner of the room, where it promptly exploded with a relatively harmless blaze of white light and an equally loud bang. Instinctively, I spun around to see if anyone had been injured. Flashcaps were really just for show and effect, but could still be dangerous at very close range. When I looked back, I caught the billow of a red cape exiting through the front door.

Clever girl.

I made my way through the startled crowd, most of whom were only now thinking of exiting the establishment. It took a few seconds for my eyes to adjust to the noonday sun. Long enough for her to make her way completely through the market and into an alleyway at the other end of the plaza. Certainly, the ability to maneuver through the crowd was merely one of her minor miracles. I ran, pushing through the throng until I reached the entrance to the alley. She was now walking, not running, a few dozen meters away. I trotted after her, trying to stay as close to the wall as possible. I have a talent for covert surveillance, but nobody can stay completely inconspicuous in broad daylight.

She rounded a corner, still very nonchalant about where she was going. Once she was out of sight, I broke into a run. I slowed just a few meters from the turn, hoping that I had made up at least a portion of the distance between us. When I crossed into the alley, a hand appeared from nowhere. Fingers wrapped themselves around my throat and threw me against the wall. Momentarily stunned by the blow, I opened my eyes and found two unmistakably blue orbs staring back at me.

"Dangerous," was all she said, and even that was little more than a mutter.

"There must be some mistake," I stuttered, not having prepared any more believable explanation.

She shook her head. "You were back at the hotel. Didn't take your eyes off me the whole time. What makes you think I'll like your offer any more than those other assholes?"

Whether from fear, shock, or some secret pressure point she held under her gloved fingers, I found it difficult to move. My muscles seemed distant things that only half-heartedly paid attention to my instructions. Her reputation as a warrior was well earned, and something about those eyes of hers seemed to compel me to tell the truth. "I was sent to find you," I managed to stutter out.

"By whom?" she demanded, her voice had the slightest trace of annoyance.

"Noto-Lib. They need to talk to you."

Her grip tightened around my neck, and her eyes darted to one side briefly as she tried to make sense of the name.

"The starport bombings last month?"

"No, that was Lib-Front." I tried to raise my arms to show that I was unarmed and meant no harm. I had been warned against carrying a weapon. The only reason I was still alive was that she didn't see me as a threat. As soon as I moved, however, an electrical current surged through the gloved fingers clasped around my throat. 

"No," she warned, almost maternally.

I gurgled in pain, holding my hands where she could see them. "We hacked orbital traffic control six weeks ago," I offered, gasping for air.

She released her hand from my neck and took a step back. I slumped against the wall, careful not to make any sudden, fatal movements.

"Insane planet," she muttered. "I can't keep your little clubs straight anymore."

"Common mistake," I offered, smiling slightly.

She nodded. "What do they want with me?"

"They know Crimson Dawn hired you to do the Ferimann assassination, then sold you out when the Feds traced it back to them."

"The Feds had help," she said matter-of-factly.

"Of course they did. Problem is, the Feds are now looking for you, so you're trying to find a back door off this planet. We can help."

"I'll find my own way off."

"We've made sure you won't--" Her hand sprang back up towards my throat in a simple warning gesture. "At least," I wheezed, trying to sound confident, but not smug, "not until you've heard our proposition."

She stiffened slightly and squinted her eyes. "I don't like being played," she hissed.

"It was the only way," I insisted. "You don't exactly have a listing on the public vid directory."

She thought about the situation for a moment, then smirked, nodded, and let go of my neck. "Set up a meeting," she demanded.

"I have a car at the trans terminal," I nodded, gesturing with my left hand.

She turned and started off, not looking back. Her cape flowed behind, adding to her already intimidating presence. I caught up to her after a few steps, not daring to look her in the eye.

"I should kill you to make an example," she muttered under her breath. I wasn't exactly sure whether I was meant to hear the words or not. "I'm setting a dangerous precedent by doing this."

It was an empty threat, probably more out of frustration at being so masterfully manipulated. She hadn't killed the men back at the bar, at least one of whom seemed ready to kill her. Since she hadn't killed me already, it was safe to assume that my superiors were right: Noto-Lib now had control of a very dangerous and powerful weapon, but somehow I felt more pity than triumph.

"It wasn't my idea," I offered.

She laughed a little and nodded. "I'd guessed that."

Lathon Industries was once the leading manufacturer of terraforming chemicals on Notosia. That was until economic instability---and a well-timed attack by one of the rebel factions---turned it into a semi-toxic slag pit. Now, the factory complex that once employed nearly ten thousand loyal Notosian workers was just another eyesore perched on the shoreline, some fifty kilometers north of Nerida City, slowly collapsing from neglect and the unceasing ravages of nature. It was the perfect place to run a revolution.

The headquarters of the Notosian Liberation Army--Noto-Lib to the people who followed politics--was conveniently located in a small power monitoring station half a kilometer underground, directly beneath one of three chemical plants. As such, it had all the necessary amenities: an independent power generator, adequate plumbing, direct communication links to the planetary data network, and multiple entry and escape routes. Its existence was one of the organization's most closely guarded secrets, even from many of its own members. As a cell leader, I had been there twice before. Most people in my position could not make that claim, but my unique background and history with the Army allowed for certain special dispensations.

The only route I knew into the complex took us across what was once the factory floor, down two different freight elevators, through at least three kilometers of unlit corridors, and finally down 15 flights of stairs to the disused generators that now served as forward guard posts. Ursza seemed calm and poised the entire time, while I, despite having far less to fear than she did, grew more and more nervous the further we descended. We waited next to the generator room's large shield doors for what seemed like an hour before a guard--satisfied that we hadn't been followed or observed--finally made their presence known.

"Chakal," the guard said. "We've been expecting you." I wasn't sure why they always say that. Of course, I was expected. One does not just walk into the headquarters of the third-largest military organization on the planet without an invitation, no matter who they are. He shot a quick glance at Ursza. "You'd be the blade dancer then. We were told you probably wouldn't surrender your weapons, but I thought I might ask politely anyway." He smiled sarcastically with a mouth only half-full of teeth.

"You may ask," Ursza replied, arching one eyebrow.

Annoyed with the show of bravado, I decided to hurry the proceedings along. "Stop wasting our time and let us in. You already said we're expected."

The guard abruptly stopped smiling and turned a grimace towards me. "I decide when you go in." His eyes scanned Ursza up and down for a moment. "I've never met a blade dancer before," he chortled, licking his lips. "Much less one as pretty as you. Always wondered if you're as tough as all those stories."

She smiled sweetly, slowly stroked his oversized bicep with one hand, and promptly laid him out cold with one jab from the other. I did my best not to snicker as she bent down and extracted the door's access card from his belt. Other voices, lacking my self-control, laughed hysterically from hidden positions in the shadows.

"Sorry about that," I tried to apologize.

"Used to it," she muttered, slamming the card into the access panel.

The door slowly slid open to reveal a well-lit reception area, formerly a control room that had been completely stripped for components. We passed through the room and into a small conference area where the deputy commander of the western division--code name: Hēi Gēzi (black dove)--sat at the head of a long wooden table. Two other men, both much older, were seated to either side of him. I had never seen either before.

"Chakal, it's always a pleasure to see one of our brothers here," he said in a formal tone, "and Good Evening to you, Blademaster Venter. It is indeed our greatest honor to have one of the Gu’Senlin as our guest." He stood up with his palms facing upwards, then clasped his hands together and bowed slightly.

Ursza repeated the same gesture and also bowed. "The honor is to serve," she recited without sincerity.

My face flushed with embarrassment. Was that considered the proper way to address a blade dancer? I suddenly wished I had been better briefed or at least done some research beforehand. Fortunately, if she took any offense at my own lack of formality, she didn't show it.

"May I present my war council? These are--for the purposes of this meeting--Kafer, my intelligence officer," he said, gesturing to the man on his right, "and Forgeran, one of my strategists." Both men stood and bowed politely. Ursza acknowledged them with only a slight nod. I, however, was truly impressed. It had been five years since signing on with the Notosian Liberation Army. Thus far, Hēi Gēzi had been the only member of the upper echelon that I had ever met personally. These three would only answer to the Chancellor himself. 

With the introductions complete, Hēi Gēzi motioned for us to be seated. "First off, Blademaster, may I express my sincere thanks for not killing Chakal here?" he chortled. 

"No point," she said flatly. I took no offense.

"I would also like to thank you for riding us of Governor Ferimann--"

"Suppose you just tell me why I'm here." Ursza's said, annoyed with the formalities.

Hēi Gēzi's jaw twitched slightly, and he clasped his hands in front of his face. "Very well." He smiled. "You've been on planet for what, at least eight months now? You've put your talents into working for five different revolutionary organizations--that we know of. To be blunt, Blademaster, we'd like you to extend the same courtesy to us." He paused, waiting for some form of acknowledgment.

She considered his words for a moment. "Go on," she said.

"You, more than anyone else on this planet, are in a very unique position. You have been inside multiple organizations, met with many key people, and worked with their strike teams. Which of us, in your opinion, stands the best chance of being the next leaders of this planet? Which of us could be victorious against a corrupt, but well-funded and well-armed government?"

"I don't care about your politics, Sir," she said.

Hēi Gēzi smiled broadly. "Indulge me then."

"Frankly, you're all poorly organized, lacking any conception of how to fight a war, much less guerrilla tactics. Your support in the population is fading, and Oron Prevo, against all odds, has actually become popular."

"Mindless rabble who are easily swayed by a handsome face and eloquent words."

"He has the backing of the media."

"Which has fed the people propaganda for so long that I'm surprised anybody really takes them seriously anymore."

"He's delivered on his promises. He's made progress."

"Not nearly enough," Hēi Gēzi slammed his hand down on the table.

"He has powerful friends who keep him well-financed."

"As do we, my dear," he smiled bitterly.

She returned the smile. "The Traders' Guild, yes. Crimson Dawn has the construction unions; Lib-Front, the technology megacorps; PMOD, the banking syndicate--"

"--Yes, you make your point," Hēi Gēzi cut her off abruptly, a slight hint of impatience in his voice. "I am more interested in your conclusions as a master warrior and strategist."

"Stalemate," she said without hesitation, "and none of you powerful enough, or popular enough, to take Prevo."

Hēi Gēzi nodded in agreement, "Not as long as we keep fighting amongst ourselves."

She considered this for a moment. "A union?"

Hēi Gēzi's smile widened. "An end to the cycle of infighting that serves no purpose but to distract us from our goals and further the aims of our common enemy."

"An ambitious plan," she said with admiration. "What does it have to do with me?"

"You are in a unique position, Blademaster. You have either worked with or been very close to the leadership of nearly all of the political organizations on this planet. As an outsider with no motivations other than obtaining your fee, you are trusted by all of them. As a blade dancer," he tapped two fingers on the table in front of him, "you have the skills needed to quickly reach all of them and extend our proposal."

My apartment is modest by most standards --- comfortable, but by no means luxurious. It was getting close to nightfall when we arrived. I turned on some lights, offered her a meal --- which she refused --- and explained that it would be a couple of hours before we could meet with my handlers.

"I hate waiting," she said, somewhat forcefully, "particularly when I'm not on the job."

"It shouldn't be too long. They'll contact me with the meeting location as soon as they're ready. In the meantime, you're welcome to take off your cloak. I know you Gu’Senlin don't like disarming, but it must be warm in that thing."

She raised an eyebrow at me, as if the fact that she was a Gu’Senlin blade dancer was some sort of secret. Even if I hadn't been told, her performance in the restaurant made it obvious. With the barest flick of her finger, the cloak unfastened and fell to the floor. She walked across the room to the balcony and stared out over the city. Her sword and scabbard were still fastened across her back. I had only seen a flash of it earlier; now I could plainly see that the blade was, in fact, curved and the hilt ornately decorated with nine symbols that I did not recognize. The scabbard, normally hidden by her cloak, was made of what appeared to be hardened leather, dyed blood-red with black-etched accents.

While I had noticed her striking beauty when I first saw her, this was the first time I'd been able to actually get a good look at her--to actually see her. Despite being a formidable warrior and cold-blooded mercenary, she was still an incredibly beautiful, exotic woman. Her body, no doubt honed to physical perfection, moved with a silent grace that accentuated her natural femininity, and it was hard to imagine any man who could not be charmed by the sound of her voice, though these too she no doubt counted as weapons in her arsenal.

"Seen enough?" she asked. I wasn't sure if she was annoyed by being stared at or trying to make a joke. My face reddened.

"It's your outfit." I scurried for anything to save myself. "It's very unusual."

"Combat suit. Neuromuscular control interface," she said, indicating the red vein-like patterns that ran across the fabric. "Picked it up on Thurin last year." She demonstrated by moving her arms and twisting her body with incredible grace. The suit responded by opening and automatically resealing several folds--hidden pouches where no doubt various tools of her trade were kept hidden, yet easily accessible when needed. A street illusionist could make a comfortable living performing tricks with a suit such as that, though it was unlikely that even the best of them could afford a cheap imitation.

"Impressive," was about all I could whistle. "And what about the sword?"

"Katana," she corrected, almost disappointed. "'Sword' is a vulgar generalization. Like calling a human an animal."

"Yeah, but is that the only weapon you use?"

"It's the only one I need," she said in a well-rehearsed fashion. "It's not the only one I carry."

"Yeah, I caught the trick with the bang cap back at the restaurant."

"She made a quick gesture with her arm, which unsealed a small pocket on her right side next to her ribcage. She extracted three small cylinders, no more than two centimeters long, and held one between each slender finger on her left hand. "The old tricks are the best," she smiled. I decided then that I liked her best when she smiled like that.

"What about guns?"

"The sword has survived as it is for 6000 years. Everyone's always trying to make a bigger and better gun. Don't like them."

"Yeah, but other people do," I said in mock scolding, remembering what had happened to the last man who dared pull a gun on her. "What do you do when people with guns are more than a sword's distance away? Bang caps are noisy, but aside from a bit of flash blindness, they don't really do any damage."

She responded by extending her left arm and pulling down on a patch of leather over her forearm to reveal a small control and display unit. She tapped two of the buttons in quick succession and a faint, barely shimmering displacement suddenly surrounded her--a personal shield generator.

I shook my head. "Yeah, that's fine with energy weapons like plazers and blasters, but it won't do much against slug throwers like the one the guy at the restaurant had."

She flicked off the PSG and stared at me, annoyed. "You're rather nosy."

"If you're going to work for us, we need to know what we're getting," I offered. Besides, you started this fashion show, I thought to myself.

She did not seem impressed. "Now it's 'us' now and not 'they'?" She was still smiling. I was beginning to think that she might actually be starting to like me. She reached behind her back, extracted a small metallic disc and handed it to me. "Be careful, it's razor sharp and almost completely frictionless."

I gingerly took the disc, struggling to find the best way to grip it as it slid around in my fingers. I was slightly heavier than it appeared, and still warm with the heat of her body. "What am I supposed to do with this?" I asked.

She walked to the far end of the room and turned back to face me. "Throw it."

I thought for a minute that she had to be kidding. I could barely grip the thing, much less throw it with any accuracy. Careful not to slice my hand open on the edges, I managed to fling it across the room towards her--considering the weight and sharpness of the disc, even my poor toss could do serious damage if somehow her little demonstration failed, but somehow I knew that she was in complete control of the situation. She held up her hand, palm outward, as if to catch the wobbling projectile. I winced, picturing the projectile slicing through her beautiful hand and embedding itself in the wall behind her, and was amazed when the disk suddenly stopped in midair and fell to the floor with a loud clang.

"Inertia shield," she explained, pointing to a small mesh of metal on the inside of her wrist. "Half a meter diameter--Aurian manufacture."

I'd heard of such things before, but tech like that was restricted for military use on Auria. The money she could get for that little piece of hardware could buy her a small moon somewhere, or probably even a large one. Aurians were a funny lot when it came down to it--pacifists mostly. One of those utopias you read about, where there is no crime because nobody wants anything. I knew for a fact this wasn't exactly true. There were tech runners and smugglers on Auria, but even they wouldn't dare think of stealing tech from the military-megacorps, definitely; their own mothers, probably; but the military, that was sacred to them. I didn't dare ask how she got her hands on that particular piece of hardware, as I was sure I wouldn't like the answer.

"I assume it takes the inertial energy of the mass passing through the field and uses it to power the rest of the suit."

"Something like that," she said as she bent over and expertly picked up the disc, twirling it between her fingers before returning it to the pocket behind her back. "I'm no tech." She shrugged. "See enough?"

"I think that, plus what you did for Crimson Dawn will satisfy anyone as to your credentials." I nodded confidently. "And I've already seen firsthand demonstration of your talents."

She ignored the last comment and busied herself padding various parts of her suit, no doubt taking an inventory of her remaining equipment. The way she moved when she did so seemed almost like a seductive dance.

I coughed nervously trying to regain my composure. "You did a great service to the people of Notosia by ridding us of Senator Ferriman--"

"Don't care about politics, Chakal" she cut me off. "It was just a job."

That struck a nerve. Could it actually be remorse? Or was it just well-rehearsed detachment? I walked back to the kitchen and poured two glasses of water from the dispenser. "Really? I thought you Gu’Senlin were supposed to be philosophers, spiritualists or artisans--not mercenaries." I said it offhandedly, not as a challenge, afraid that she might find it more of an accusation instead of genuine curiosity.

"There are five tastes," she intoned rhythmically, "but more flavors than can ever be tasted. Haven't found mine yet."

"Yeah, but do you enjoy this? Killing people for money, I mean?" I handed her one of the glasses which she took, reluctantly. 

She gingerly took a sip of water. "It's not about joy. It's survival."

I found her choice of words curious. What, after all, could threaten someone with such talents at her disposal? "So, you're saying you'd go and work for the government just as easily as you'd work for us?"

"If they paid me," she answered matter-of-factly.

For some reason, that thought angered me. "Oron Prevo is megalomaniacal, power-mad--"

"Ever met him?"

"I don't have to. He's a politician like all the others. He assumed power with the help of powerful friends, and now he's their puppet. He doesn't care about the people."

She rolled her eyes and chuckled softly. "In hostile territory, you stick with your allies. It's called survival." The tone was mocking.

"I thought you didn't care about our politics."

"I don't, but this planet is insane," she laughed. "You've gone through thirty governments in two centuries. Political revolt is a way of life for you people."

"Not revolt," I countered, "revolution."

"Propaganda, bunk," she answered defiantly, but without emotion.

I suppose the debate would have gone on all night, and I must admit I was finding an outsider's point of view fascinating. Fortunately, my com panel beeped with a new message. The first enemy resistance movement was ready to meet with Ursza Venter. Whether she was interested in or even agreed with our politics, she was about to become deeply involved in them.
